Meta Internship

Internal search engine design system for Meta employees. NDA restricts visual assets, but the thinking is all here.

My Role

Product Designer (Intern)

Team

Tarjni (User Researcher)

Duration

5 weeks

Tools

Figma, Meta Design System

The problem

Meta employees designing and programming pages for the company's internal search engine were running into the same problems repeatedly: inconsistent layouts, reliance on non-standard HTML and inline CSS, and high cognitive load when reading dense, unstructured pages. There was no shared template — everyone was reinventing the wheel, badly.

My task was to define what a standardized site template should look like: one that could scale across the breadth of existing internal sites while reducing the technical overhead on engineers.

Research: auditing 46 live sites

Before sketching anything, my researcher Tarjni and I catalogued 46 existing Meta internal search engine sites. We sorted patterns into two buckets: what was working (and worth keeping) and what was actively hurting usability.

Patterns worth keeping:

  • Modern visual design — hero imagery, clean hierarchy
  • Team identity elements — logos and branding that made sites feel owned
  • Componentized layouts — cards and accordions that scaled well

Patterns to eliminate:

  • Walls of unbroken text with no visual breathing room
  • Null data states — broken layouts when content was missing
  • Inconsistent hierarchy — unclear what was a heading vs. body vs. action

Design principles I committed to

01

Company guidelines first

02

Component-driven

03

Accessible by default

04

Cognitive load reduction

The hardest design challenge: grid systems

The biggest friction point in the project wasn't visual — it was structural. Applying Meta's standard column grid in a way that would genuinely work across the full range of existing sites required multiple iterations. Most sites had wildly different content volumes. The grid needed to be flexible enough to handle both a dense documentation page and a sparse landing page without breaking.

I worked through multiple grid variations in Figma, testing each against a set of representative existing sites. The final approach used a modified 12-column system with defined responsive breakpoints that allowed subgrids at the section level — meaning individual page sections could adapt their density without disrupting the overall layout.

Outcome

The final template shipped internally during my internship and was adopted as the standard for Meta's internal search engine sites. The template covered a landing page pattern and multiple sub-page variants, all built on cards, accordions, and Meta's existing component library — reducing the need for custom HTML or inline CSS workarounds.

1000s Employees affected by the template

What I'd do differently

Five weeks was tight. I wish I'd had time to run structured usability testing with employees who would actually use the template — not just collect feedback informally. I also leaned heavily on the audit data to make decisions, which was efficient but meant some choices were inferred rather than validated. Given more time, I would have run at least two rounds of concept testing before finalizing the grid system.

BVCC Mental Health App

An 8-week research and design sprint to identify college students' core challenges and design a community-based solution.

My Role

UX Researcher + Designer

Methods

User interviews, card sorting

Duration

8 weeks

Tools

Figma, Sketch

The brief

The challenge was deliberately open-ended: "Identify a universal conflict for college students and create a digital solution that facilitates growth." No predefined problem, no obvious starting point. The entire project direction had to emerge from research.

Starting with questions, not assumptions

Before interviewing anyone, I wrote out five research questions to keep the work from drifting into generic territory:

  • How does mental health impact academic performance?
  • How can stress be alleviated to improve "external performance"?
  • What motivates the average college student?
  • Which self-improvement tools (apps, planners) are most popular — and why?
  • Where do students struggle internally, beyond just academics?

What 4 interviews revealed

I interviewed four college students with one-on-one sessions. Some key quotes that shaped the direction:

  • "I am still trying to figure out my system" — the absence of any routine was the pain, not a broken routine
  • "The distracting college environment" — the problem isn't self-discipline, it's context
  • "Journaling, trying not to overthink things" — students had instincts, they just needed structure
  • "A lot less stressed and a lot more productive" — the definition of success was remarkably consistent

The through-line: stress is the umbrella. Students were struggling not just with time management but with the sudden absence of external structure that school had always provided. College was the first time they had to govern themselves — and most didn't have the tools.

Problem statement

College students face challenges with time management, balancing responsibilities, and creating routines they can sustain — because stress undermines their ability to self-regulate, and the college environment actively works against structure-building.

Design direction: community over gamification

Most mental health apps lean on individual tracking and gamification. My research pointed in a different direction: students wanted connection. Accountability from peers, not from an app's streak counter. The solution became a community forum where students could share routines, get support, and reduce the isolation that amplifies stress.

Key design principles I applied:

01

Low-friction onboarding

02

Community-first

03

Minimal cognitive load

04

Safe, non-judgmental tone

Outcome & honest reflection

The project delivered a high-fidelity mobile prototype covering onboarding, community forum, and routine-tracking features. The research foundation was strong enough that every design decision traced back to a specific interview insight.

What I'd do with more time: concept testing. I designed to the evidence I had, but I didn't close the loop with validation from users. In hindsight, even informal guerrilla testing — showing the prototype to 3–4 students and watching them use it — would have surfaced usability issues I couldn't see because I was too close to the work. That's the step I'd build in from day one on any future project.
