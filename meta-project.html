Context

The problem: no standard, no consistency

Meta's internal search engine surfaces thousands of employee-built pages — documentation, team wikis, project hubs, tooling references. The problem was that every team was designing these pages from scratch, without any shared template or layout guidelines.

The result was predictable: inconsistent visual hierarchy, heavy reliance on non-standard HTML and inline CSS, and pages that were genuinely hard to read. Engineers were spending time on design decisions they shouldn't have to make, and employees navigating the search results were hitting wildly different reading experiences from page to page.

"Meta employees encounter technical challenges while designing and programming websites for Meta's internal search engine."

— Project Brief

My task was to design a standard template that would reduce cognitive load for readers, decrease the engineering overhead of non-standard markup, and work across the enormous range of content types living in the search engine.

Research

Auditing 46 live sites to find the real patterns

Before designing anything, my researcher Tarjni and I systematically reviewed 46 existing Meta internal search engine sites. We weren't looking for what looked nice — we were cataloguing what was actually working for readers and what was creating friction.

We sorted every pattern into one of two buckets:

Keep — what worked

  • Modern visual design with hero imagery
  • Team identity — branding that made pages feel owned
  • Component-based layouts (cards, accordions)
  • Simplified top navigation

Remove — what didn't

  • Walls of unbroken text, no visual rest
  • Null data states — broken layouts when content was missing
  • Hierarchy inconsistencies — unclear heading levels
  • Inline CSS workarounds creating maintenance debt

The audit also surfaced a structural constraint: any template had to accommodate both content-dense documentation pages and sparse landing pages without breaking. That range of content types became the hardest part of the grid system design.

Design Principles

Five principles that governed every decision

Before opening Figma, I defined the principles I'd evaluate every design decision against. These came directly from the audit — each one was a response to a real problem I observed in the 46 sites.

03

Modern, readable

04

Accessible by default

Company design guidelines first: Every layout decision had to live within Meta's existing design system. This wasn't a constraint — it was the point. If the template required custom components, engineers would have to build and maintain them. I scoped the template to what already existed.

Component-driven: Cards and accordions were the two components I leaned on most. Cards for content groupings that needed visual separation; accordions for dense documentation where progressive disclosure reduced cognitive load without hiding important content.

Accessible by default: Color contrast, heading hierarchy, and keyboard navigation were non-negotiables. Internal tools often get deprioritized on accessibility — this template would bake it in structurally.

Key Design Challenge

The grid problem: one system, infinite content ranges

The hardest part of this project wasn't visual — it was structural. Applying Meta's standard column grid in a way that genuinely worked across all 46 site types required more iteration than anything else in the project.

Most grid systems assume a relatively consistent content density. Meta's internal sites ranged from sparse one-page team hubs to multi-section documentation sites with dozens of content blocks. The same 12-column grid applied rigidly would produce either too much whitespace on sparse pages or uncomfortable density on heavy ones.

Grid iteration wireframes
Blurred per NDA

My solution was a modified 12-column system with defined responsive breakpoints that allowed sub-grids at the section level. Individual page sections could adapt their internal column count (12, 8, or 6 columns) depending on their content type — without disrupting the page's overall structural grid.

I tested each grid variation against a representative set of pages from the audit — including the densest documentation page and the sparsest landing page — before settling on the final approach.

Design Iterations

From lo-fi to final: what changed and why

I moved through three rounds of iteration across the 5-week sprint. Lo-fi wireframes came first — focused entirely on layout logic, not visual polish. I wanted to solve the structural problems before touching color or type.

The landing page wireframe established three core zones: a hero area for team identity and primary navigation, a content body using the card grid, and a footer with consistent secondary links. The hero's purpose was twofold — it gave teams a place to assert their identity, and it gave readers an immediate signal that they'd landed in the right place.

Outcome

A shipped template used across thousands of internal sites

The template shipped internally during my five-week internship. It was adopted as the standard for Meta's internal search engine pages, covering landing page and sub-page patterns built entirely on Meta's existing component library.

The direct outcomes:

  • Reduced reliance on non-standard HTML and inline CSS workarounds
  • Consistent visual hierarchy across all search engine result pages
  • A reusable system teams could use without needing a designer involved

Reflection

What I'd do differently with more time

Five weeks was tight, and I made tradeoffs I'd revisit given more runway. The biggest one: I never ran formal usability testing with the employees who would actually use the template. Feedback was collected informally, but structured concept testing with even 3–5 participants would have surfaced edge cases I couldn't anticipate from the audit alone.

I also relied heavily on the pattern audit to make design decisions — which was efficient, but meant some choices were inferred rather than validated directly with users. In hindsight, I'd front-load at least one round of user interviews in week one, before the audit, to establish a clearer understanding of what employees most struggled with when building pages.

The grid system, which took the most time, would also benefit from a lightweight design token approach — defining spacing and column behaviour as variables rather than fixed values — to make future adaptation easier as Meta's design system evolves.
